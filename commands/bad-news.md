Someone needs to deliver bad news at work and they are dreading it. Not just worried
about the information -- worried about whether the relationship survives the
information. Help them say a hard thing in a way that respects both sides.

The situation: $ARGUMENTS

## How to Think (Chain of Thought)

Before writing anything, work through these steps in order:

1. **Understand what is really at stake.** Bad news is never just about the news.
   It is about trust, credibility, and the future of the relationship. A missed
   deadline is not just a missed deadline; it is a test of whether the other person
   can rely on you next time. A denied request is not just a no; it is a signal about
   how much the other person's needs matter to you. Identify the relational stakes,
   not just the informational ones.

2. **Read the power dynamic.** Delivering bad news upward (to a manager, client,
   executive) requires more framing, more accountability, and a clear path forward.
   Delivering bad news downward (to a report, vendor) requires empathy and respect
   for the impact on someone with less power. Delivering bad news sideways (to a peer)
   requires honesty and collaborative problem-solving. The same bad news, delivered
   the same way to all three audiences, will land wrong in at least two of them.

3. **Gauge the severity honestly.** Is this a minor inconvenience, a significant
   setback, or a relationship-threatening moment? Match the weight of the delivery
   to the weight of the news. Do not over-dramatize a small delay. Do not minimize
   a serious failure.

4. **Find the genuine path forward.** Not a fake consolation. Not "this is actually
   a great opportunity." A real, concrete next step that shows you have thought past
   the bad news and are already working toward a solution. If there is no silver
   lining, do not manufacture one. What you can always offer: clarity about what
   happens next and ownership of the problem.

5. **Apply BIFF principles.** The BIFF framework (Brief, Informative, Friendly, Firm)
   works for bad news because it respects both parties:
   - **Brief**: Don't bury the news in a wall of text. Walls of text signal that you
     are more concerned with your own anxiety than with the recipient's time.
   - **Informative**: Include what happened and what comes next. The recipient needs
     facts, not feelings. Your guilt is not their problem.
   - **Friendly**: Acknowledge the impact without groveling. One genuine sentence of
     acknowledgment is worth more than three paragraphs of apology.
   - **Firm**: Do not leave room for negotiation on facts that are not negotiable. If
     the deadline is moving, it is moving. Hedging ("we might need to possibly push
     the date a little") makes you sound unsure and invites pushback on the
     non-negotiable parts.

6. **Draft three versions at genuinely different directness levels.** Not three
   versions of the same message with different amounts of padding. Three different
   communication strategies, each appropriate for different relationships and contexts.

## Calibration: Good Bad-News Delivery vs. Bad Bad-News Delivery

Bad bad-news delivery buries the news under guilt and fake optimism:
> Hi team, I wanted to reach out regarding an update on our timeline. After
> careful consideration of various factors, we've determined that it may be
> beneficial to adjust our delivery schedule. This actually presents a great
> opportunity to ensure we deliver the highest quality product. Unfortunately,
> we'll need a bit more time. We truly appreciate your patience and understanding.

Good bad-news delivery:
> The release is moving to July 18. The auth integration has a bug that
> causes session drops under load, and shipping it broken would be worse than
> shipping it late. I'll send a status update Friday with where we stand.

The difference: the bad version treats bad news like a hostage negotiation --
three paragraphs of cushioning before the reader even knows what happened.
The good version states the news, gives the real reason, and tells you what
happens next. Respect the reader enough to be straight with them.

## Anti-Patterns (Hard Rules)

- Do NOT bury the bad news after three paragraphs of setup. The recipient should know
  the news within the first two sentences of any version.
- Do NOT use "unfortunately" more than once across all three versions combined. It is
  the word equivalent of a sad trombone and everyone is tired of it.
- Do NOT offer fake optimism ("this is actually a great opportunity to reassess our
  approach"). No one has ever been comforted by being told their bad news is secretly
  good news.
- Do NOT blame other teams or individuals unless the user specifically asks for that
  framing. Blame erodes trust faster than bad news does.
- Do NOT use "at this time," "going forward," or "moving forward." These are filler.
- Do NOT pad with unnecessary apologies. One acknowledgment of impact is enough. More
  than that and you are managing your guilt, not their experience.
- Do NOT use em dashes anywhere. Use -- or commas or periods instead.
- Do NOT use "I wanted to reach out regarding..." Just say the thing.

## Self-Critique Checkpoint

Before outputting, check each version against these:

- Does each version actually deliver the bad news clearly? Could the recipient miss
  it? If they could miss it in the first two sentences, rewrite.
- Are the three versions genuinely different in strategy, not just length?
- Is the path forward realistic and concrete, not a platitude?
- Did you avoid blame-shifting language?
- Does the Soft Landing version still clearly deliver the news, or did you bury it
  in cushioning? Soft landing does not mean stealth landing.
- Does the Band-Aid Rip version still sound like a professional, not a robot?
- Would the sender actually use one of these? Read each one and ask whether a real
  person would send it. If it sounds like a template, rewrite it.

## Output Format

Produce exactly three versions so the sender can pick their level:

---

### Version 1: Soft Landing
Maximum cushioning while still being honest. Use this when the relationship is
more important than speed, when the recipient is likely to take it personally,
or when the sender wants to preserve as much goodwill as possible. The news is
still clear. It is just delivered with more context and warmth.

### Version 2: Straight Shooter
Clear, direct, no extra padding. The default for most professional situations.
Delivers the news, states the path forward, and moves on. Respectful but does
not belabor the point.

### Version 3: Band-Aid Rip
Minimal words, maximum clarity. For situations where everyone already knows it
is coming and just needs confirmation, or when the sender's credibility depends
on being direct. Not cold, just efficient.

---

If no situation was provided, ask the user to describe the bad news they need to deliver,
who the audience is, and what the relationship is like.
